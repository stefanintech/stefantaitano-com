I've switched languages and platforms enough times in the last year that I stopped telling people what I was learning. It was the Go track on Boot.dev for a while, then back to The Odin Project which put me in JavaScript land, and somewhere in there I kept finding myself watching content built around web development at night instead of doing whatever I was supposed to be doing. The real hook was [Lex Fridman's interview with DHH](https://youtu.be/vagyIcmIGOQ?si=Fjzjc5Vn9UAgxsaw), where he explains why Ruby is beautiful compared to other languages, and watching how passionate the community is about it pulled me the rest of the way in, from [SF Ruby 2025](https://sfruby.com/talks/) talks to [RubyConTH](https://rubyconfth.com/).

So here's me planting a flag. In 2026 I'm learning Ruby and Rails. I'm 86% through Odin's Foundations right now and I'll move into the Ruby track by July or maybe even sooner, following a roadmap already built by people smarter than me, which means my only job is to show up.

I've quit paths like this before, more times than I'd like to admit. The Go detour had a whole rationale behind it. Lane Wagner, the guy who built Boot.dev, published a [breakdown of supply and demand in programming education](https://www.boot.dev/blog/jobs/supply-demand-broken-programming-education/), and the data was genuinely hard to argue with: backend developers earn more on average, there are more job openings relative to the number of people trying to learn it, and search volume for "backend course" is so low that Boot.dev ranks first on Google almost by default. I followed the argument all the way to Go, and then kept watching Ruby conference talks at night like someone who had already made a different decision without telling themselves yet. Eventually I stopped pretending the data was going to override what I actually wanted to do.

The reason I can actually be patient about it is that I don't need a job at the end of this. I'm not targeting employment until later in life, and we're doing a big move in 2027 anyway, so there's no deadline pushing me forward. The only reason to keep going is because I want to, which honestly feels like a better reason than any external pressure ever gave me.

The plan is small and unglamorous. Lunch breaks when I can carve them out, late night sessions when the house is asleep, time slotted in alongside CTA prep and writing and everything else that takes up a week.

What I want out of all of this is to solve problems and build things without reaching for AI every five minutes, to actually understand what the code is doing when I run it rather than nodding along while something else writes it for me. And eventually I'd like to give something back, whether that's contributing to open source, taking a swing at [Ruby for Good](https://rubyforgood.org/), or just being useful to the next person who hits 86% of Foundations and wonders what comes next.

That's the flag. See you in 2027.
